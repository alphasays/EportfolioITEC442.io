A fully digital enterprise

A fully digital enterprise refers to an organization that has successfully transformed its operations, processes, and activities by leveraging digital technologies and tools, also known as digital transformation. In such an enterprise, digital technologies are deeply integrated into every aspect of the organization's operations, enabling increased efficiency, agility, and innovation with access to real-time data. This consists of various digital features such as:

  • Digital Infrastructure: A combination of hardware, software, networks, and cloud-based systems necessary to support digital operations and achieve a full digital enterprise.
  • Digital Process Automation: Replacing or automating traditional manual and paper-based processes with digital solutions, including areas such as document management, workflow automation, and data analytics, which contributes to data-driven decision making.
  • Digital Customer Experience: Utilizing Customer Relationship Management (CRM) systems to improve interaction and enhance satisfaction through personalized marketing.
  • Cybersecurity and Data Privacy: Ensuring the safe storage and protection of data with the right policies and security measures in place, as the fundamental part of a digital enterprise.

Examples of fully digital enterprises that I am particularly experienced with include the Integrated Financial Management Information System (IFMIS) for public finance management used in the Gambia, Kenya, Ethiopia and every country in Latin America has an IFMIS system for state funds, payroll and other governemnt activites. Another one is the Diplomatic Portal developed by the Turkish Government. These systems demonstrate how digital technologies can streamline operations, improve efficiency, and provide enhanced services in sectors like finance and diplomacy.


While digital transformation offers numerous benefits, it also poses certain cybersecurity challenges and concerns. Some of these challenges include:

  1. Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs): Sophisticated and prolonged cyber-attacks aimed at gaining unauthorized access to a network, which can target valuable data and interconnected systems in a fully digital enterprise.
  2. Insider Threats: Employees, contractors, or partners who may intentionally or inadvertently compromise data security, potentially through theft or leakage of sensitive information or falling victim to social engineering attacks.
  3. Data Breaches: Cybercriminals targeting fully digital enterprises to gain unauthorized access to sensitive information, leading to financial losses, reputational damage, and regulatory penalties.
  4. Cloud Security: Securing cloud environments, considering issues such as misconfiguration, insecure APIs, data breaches due to shared infrastructure, and data loss or leakage, especially in regions with limited power or internet stability.
  5. Mobile Security: Additional security risks introduced by the pervasive use of mobile devices in a fully digital enterprise, including data exposure, malware infections, and unauthorized access if not adequately managed and secured.
  6. IoT Vulnerabilities: The potential vulnerabilities introduced by Internet of Things (IoT) devices and sensors used in a fully digital enterprise, which can be exploited by attackers to gain unauthorized access or disrupt operations.
  7. Compliance and Regulations: The need for fully digital enterprises to comply with industry regulations and data protection laws, while maintaining strong cybersecurity measures.
  8. Third-Party Risks: The cybersecurity risks associated with third-party vendors, partners, and suppliers, which need to be assessed and managed in a network of interconnected systems.

For a bricks and mortar SME (Small and Medium-sized Enterprise) looking to become a digital enterprise, there are specific cybersecurity challenges to consider:


  1. Limited Resources: SMEs often struggle with limited budgets and resources, which can make it harder to invest in strong cybersecurity measures, including hiring security personnel and IT experts.
  2. Employee Awareness and Training: Ensuring that employees are aware of cybersecurity threats and best practices is crucial for reducing attacks, including insider threats, social engineering, and phishing attempts.
  3. Old and Obsolete Systems and Infrastructure: Limited resources may make it challenging for SMEs to upgrade to modern systems with good infrastructure, potentially introducing vulnerabilities if proper security controls are not implemented.
  4. Third-Party Vendors and Supply Chain Risk: SMEs often rely on third-party vendors and suppliers, introducing potential vulnerabilities that need to be addressed to stay safe from hacks and ensure security beyond their own applications.
  5. Data Protection and Privacy: Establishing proper data protection and privacy measures becomes essential as SMEs collect and store more digital data, requiring compliance with data protection regulations.
  6. Cloud Adoption: Embracing cloud services introduces a different set of security considerations, requiring careful evaluation of cloud providers, understanding shared responsibility models, and implementing proper configurations and access controls.
  7. Scalability and Flexibility: Balancing growth and scalability with robust cybersecurity practices can be a challenge for SMEs during their digital transformation journey.

In summary, achieving a fully digital enterprise brings numerous benefits, but it also requires addressing cybersecurity challenges and considering the specific concerns faced by different types of organizations, such as SMEs.

PCI DSS Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard

This global security standard for information is designed to enhance control over credit card data to prevent fraud. All businesses regardless of size must follow PCI DSS requirements if they accept credit card payments from the five major brands. Those brands are American Express, Discover, MasterCard, Visa, and the Japan Credit Bureau (JCB). For any organization that processes, stores, or transmits cardholder and payment data, PCI DSS compliance is required.
